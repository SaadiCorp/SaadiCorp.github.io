Chapter 1: Introduction to Economics

What is Economics?

Economics is the study of how individuals, firms, and governments make choices under scarcity—a situation where limited resources must be allocated among competing wants.

There are two main branches:

  • Microeconomics: Focuses on individual agents (households, firms) and markets.
  • Macroeconomics: Focuses on the economy as a whole (GDP, inflation, unemployment).

Core Concepts

1. Scarcity

Every society has limited resources. Scarcity forces choices—this is the central economic problem.

2. Choice

Due to scarcity, agents must make choices about how to use their resources.

3. Opportunity Cost

The value of the next-best alternative foregone when a decision is made.

Example:

If a government spends PKR 1 billion on military equipment, the opportunity cost could be fewer hospitals or schools.

Types of Economic Systems

NormativeBased on value judgments or opinions.“The government should reduce unemployment.”

Economic Agents

  • Households: Consume goods/services, provide labor.
  • Firms: Produce goods/services using factors of production.
  • Governments: Regulate and intervene in markets.
  • The Foreign Sector: Trades with other countries (exports/imports).

  • All economies face trade-offs and must decide how to use limited resources efficiently.

Chapter 2: The Economic Problem & Opportunity Cost

What is the Basic Economic Problem?

At the heart of all economic theory lies one fundamental issue: scarcity.

Scarcity means that while human wants are unlimited, resources are limited.

This gives rise to the basic economic questions every society must answer:

  1. What to produce?
  1. How to produce?
  1. For whom to produce?

These questions determine the allocation of resources across different uses.

Opportunity Cost in Depth

Definition:

Opportunity cost is the value of the next-best alternative forgone when a choice is made.

Example:

You choose to spend 3 hours studying economics instead of working a part-time job that pays PKR 600. The opportunity cost is PKR 600.

Opportunity cost helps individuals, businesses, and governments make rational decisions.

Production Possibility Frontier (PPF)

The PPF is a curve that shows the maximum combinations of two goods or services an economy can produce when resources are fully and efficiently utilized.

Movement Along vs. Shift of the PPF

ConceptDescriptionExample
Movement AlongReallocation of resources from one good to another.More tanks, fewer tractors.
Shift OutwardEconomic growth (e.g., technology, capital investment).Increase in productivity.
Shift InwardNegative shock (e.g., war, natural disaster).Loss of resources.

Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost

As production of one good increases, the opportunity cost of producing additional units also increases. This leads to a bowed-outward PPF shape.

Why? Resources are not equally efficient in producing all goods.

Specialization and Trade

Specialization occurs when individuals or countries focus on producing goods/services they are best at.

Trade allows countries to consume beyond their PPF by exchanging surplus output.

Comparative Advantage: When one party can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another.

Economic Efficiency

  • Productive Efficiency: Producing on the PPF (no wasted resources).
  • Allocative Efficiency: Producing the right mix of goods society values most.

Chapter Summary

  • All societies must allocate limited resources among competing needs.
  • Opportunity cost is a key tool for rational decision-making.
  • The PPF illustrates scarcity, trade-offs, and efficiency.
  • Economic growth shifts the PPF outward.
  • Specialization and trade improve overall welfare through comparative advantage.

Chapter 3: Demand, Supply & Market Equilibrium

What is a Market?

A market is any place—physical or virtual—where buyers and sellers come together to exchange goods and services.

Examples: Grocery stores, online marketplaces, financial exchanges (like PSX).

Markets determine prices and quantities through the interaction of demand and supply.

Part 1: Demand

Definition of Demand

Demand refers to the quantity of a good or service that consumers are willing and able to buy at different prices, over a given period of time.

Law of Demand

As price falls, quantity demanded rises, and as price rises, quantity demanded falls — ceteris paribus (all else equal).

This gives the demand curve a downward slope.

Causes of a Shift in Demand (Non-price Factors)

FactorEffect on Demand
IncomeHigher income → more demand (normal goods); less for inferior goods
Tastes & PreferencesFavorable trends → increased demand
Prices of Related GoodsSubstitutes (e.g., Pepsi vs. Coke); Complements (e.g., tea & sugar)
ExpectationsIf prices expected to rise, current demand increases
PopulationLarger population → higher market demand
A change in price causes movement along the curve.

A change in non-price factors causes the curve to shift.

Part 2: Supply

Definition of Supply

Supply is the quantity of a good or service that producers are willing and able to sell at different prices over a given time period.

Law of Supply

As price rises, quantity supplied rises and vice versa — ceteris paribus.

This gives the supply curve an upward slope.

Causes of a Shift in Supply (Non-price Factors)

FactorEffect on Supply
Input CostsLower input costs → increased supply
TechnologyAdvances → more efficient production → higher supply
Taxes & SubsidiesHigher taxes → lower supply; subsidies → increased supply
ExpectationsIf future prices expected to rise → current supply may fall
Number of SellersMore sellers → greater market supply
A price change = movement along the curve.

A non-price factor = shift of the curve.

Part 3: Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium

The point at which quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

This determines the market price and quantity traded.

ConceptDescription
Equilibrium Price (P*)The price at which Qd = Qs
Equilibrium Quantity (Q*)The quantity exchanged at P*

Disequilibrium

ConditionDescriptionResult
Surplus (Excess Supply)Price is too high → Qs > QdDownward pressure on price
Shortage (Excess Demand)Price is too low → Qd > QsUpward pressure on price

Part 4: Government Interventions

Price Ceilings (Maximum Price)

  • Set below equilibrium.
  • Leads to shortages.
  • Example: Rent control.

Price Floors (Minimum Price)

  • Set above equilibrium.
  • Leads to surpluses.
  • Example: Minimum wage.

Chapter Summary

  • Demand and supply determine market prices and quantities.
  • Demand has an inverse relationship with price; supply has a direct one.
  • Shifts in curves are caused by non-price factors.
  • Equilibrium ensures there’s no excess demand or supply.
  • Government interventions can distort markets and cause imbalances.

Chapter 4: Elasticity of Demand and Supply

Elasticity measures how responsive one variable is to a change in another.

In economics, the most common types of elasticity are:

  • Income Elasticity of Demand (YED)
  • Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand (XED)

Each type helps analyze consumer and producer behavior in response to changes in price, income, or related goods.

Chapter 5: Consumer and Producer Theory

This chapter explores the rational behavior of consumers and producers, focusing on how they make decisions to maximize utility or profit, respectively, within constraints.

Part 1: Consumer Theory

Utility: The Foundation of Consumer Choice

Utility is the satisfaction or pleasure a consumer derives from consuming a good or service.

Types of Utility

TypeExplanation
Total Utility (TU)The total satisfaction from consuming all units of a good
Marginal Utility (MU)The additional satisfaction from consuming one more unit

Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

As a person consumes more units of a good, the marginal utility derived from each additional unit declines, holding everything else constant.
Example: The first slice of pizza gives high satisfaction; by the 4th or 5th slice, satisfaction per slice decreases.

Consumer Equilibrium

Consumer equilibrium occurs when the marginal utility per rupee spent on each good is equalized across all goods.

This is when the consumer gets the maximum utility for their budget.

Budget Constraint

Consumers face limited income, which creates a budget line representing all combinations of two goods they can afford.

Key Concepts:

  • Inside the line: Affordable but not maximizing utility
  • On the line: Affordable and fully using budget
  • Outside the line: Unaffordable

Indifference Curves

An indifference curve represents combinations of goods that provide the same level of utility.

Properties:

  • Downward sloping
  • Convex to the origin
  • Indifference curves never intersect
  • Higher curves represent higher satisfaction

Optimal Consumption

Occurs at the point where the budget line is tangent to the highest possible indifference curve.

Part 2: Producer Theory

Objective: Profit Maximization

Firms aim to maximize profit, where:

Production Function

Shows the relationship between inputs and output.

Where:

  • K = Capital

Law of Diminishing Returns

As more of a variable input (e.g., labor) is added to a fixed input (e.g., machinery), output will increase at a decreasing rate.

A firm maximizes profit where:
  • If MR > MC → Produce more
  • If MR < MC → Produce less
  • If MR = MC → Profit is maximized

Chapter Summary

  • Consumers aim to maximize utility within a budget; firms aim to maximize profits.
  • Diminishing marginal utility explains demand behavior.
  • Producers face costs, diminishing returns, and revenue trade-offs.
  • Both consumers and producers respond to prices, constrained by income and resources.

Chapter 6: Market Structures

Market structures describe the competitive environment in which firms operate. These structures affect pricing, output, efficiency, and overall consumer welfare.

There are four main types of market structures:

  1. Perfect Competition
  1. Monopolistic Competition
  1. Oligopoly
  1. Monopoly

1. Perfect Competition

Characteristics:

  • Many buyers and sellers
  • Identical (homogeneous) products
  • Free entry and exit
  • Perfect information
  • No control over price (price takers)

Graphical Representation:

  • The firm’s demand curve is perfectly elastic (horizontal) at the market price.
  • Equilibrium occurs where MR = MC.

Long-Run Outcome:

  • Normal profits only (TR = TC)
  • Allocative efficiency: P = MC
  • Productive efficiency: Minimum of AC
Example: Agricultural markets (e.g., wheat, rice in competitive global markets)

2. Monopolistic Competition

Characteristics:

  • Many sellers, but each sells a slightly differentiated product
  • Free entry and exit
  • Some control over price (price makers to a degree)
  • Advertising and branding play a key role

Short Run vs. Long Run

PeriodOutcome
Short RunFirms can earn abnormal profits or losses
Long RunNew entrants eliminate abnormal profits; only normal profits remain

Efficiency:

  • Not allocatively efficient: P > MC
  • Not productively efficient: Output is not at minimum AC
Example: Clothing brands, cafes, restaurants

3. Oligopoly

Characteristics:

  • Few large firms dominate the market
  • High barriers to entry
  • Interdependence: Firms closely monitor competitors
  • Possibility of collusion
  • Products may be homogeneous or differentiated

Key Models of Oligopoly:

Kinked Demand Curve

  • Assumes firms will not follow price increases, but will follow price cuts, creating a kink in the demand curve.
  • Leads to price rigidity.

Collusive Oligopoly

  • Firms form a cartel and agree on price/output.
  • Often illegal in many countries (e.g., OPEC’s informal cartel).

Game Theory (Optional Advanced Concept)

Oligopolies often use strategic behavior, where firms anticipate competitor actions (e.g., price wars, loyalty discounts).

Example: Coke vs. Pepsi, telecom operators

4. Monopoly

Characteristics:

  • Single seller, no close substitutes
  • Complete control over price (price maker)
  • High barriers to entry
  • Downward-sloping demand curve

Sources of Monopoly Power:

  • Legal barriers (e.g., patents, copyrights)
  • Control over essential resources
  • Natural monopolies (e.g., electricity grids)
  • Government licensing

Pricing and Output:

  • Produces where MR = MC
  • Charges price from the demand curve
  • P > MC → Not allocatively efficient
  • May have deadweight loss

Evaluation of Monopoly:

PositiveNegative
Economies of scaleHigher prices
Innovation & R&DLower output
Long-term investmentInefficiency due to lack of competition

Chapter Summary

  • Market structures affect how firms price, produce, and compete.
  • Perfect competition is most efficient; monopoly is least.
  • Monopolistic competition combines features of monopoly and perfect competition.
  • Oligopolies are strategic and interdependent.

Chapter 7: The Role of Government in Economics

Government plays a critical role in ensuring that markets operate fairly, efficiently, and in alignment with societal goals. In cases of market failure, government intervention becomes essential.

Part 2: Public Goods

Characteristics

  • Non-excludable: No one can be excluded from using them
  • Non-rivalrous: One person’s use does not reduce availability for others
Examples: National defense, lighthouses, public fireworks displays

Free Rider Problem

Because individuals can consume public goods without paying, private firms have no incentive to produce them — leading to underproduction.

Solution: Government provides public goods funded by taxation.

Chapter 8: Macroeconomics Overview – GDP, Unemployment, and Inflation

Monetary policy refers to the actions taken by a central bank to control the money supply, interest rates, and credit availability in the economy to achieve macroeconomic objectives such as price stability, full employment, and economic growth.

In Pakistan, monetary policy is conducted by the State Bank of Pakistan (SBP). In the US, it is the Federal Reserve; in the UK, the Bank of England.

Part 2: Tools of Monetary Policy

Central banks use monetary policy instruments to control liquidity and steer the economy.

1. Policy Interest Rate (Discount Rate / Base Rate)

  • Changing the policy rate affects the cost of borrowing and saving.
  • Lower interest rates encourage borrowing and investment.
  • Higher interest rates discourage borrowing and slow inflation.
In Pakistan, this is the SBP policy rate, set at Monetary Policy Committee meetings.

2. Open Market Operations (OMOs)

  • The central bank buys or sells government securities in the open market.
  • Buying securities injects money → expansionary
  • Selling securities withdraws money → contractionary

3. Reserve Requirements (Cash Reserve Ratio - CRR)

  • Banks must keep a portion of deposits as reserves with the central bank.
  • Lower CRR → more money to lend → expansion
  • Higher CRR → restricts credit creation → contraction

4. Moral Suasion

  • The central bank advises or pressures banks to act in line with policy goals.
  • Not legally binding but influential.

  1. Policy Rate Changes
  1. Affects market interest rates (bank loans, mortgages)

  1. Impacts aggregate demand

An independent central bank is free from political influence, allowing it to focus on long-term stability rather than short-term populism.

Part 3: Dividend Theories

1. Dividend Irrelevance Theory (Modigliani & Miller)

In a perfect capital market, dividend policy does not affect firm value.

Assumptions:

  • No taxes
  • No transaction costs
  • Rational investors
  • Investment policy unaffected by dividend policy

Investors can create “homemade dividends” by selling shares.

2. Bird-in-the-Hand Theory (Gordon & Lintner)

Investors prefer certain dividends today over uncertain capital gains in the future.

  • Dividends reduce investor uncertainty
  • Higher dividends → higher value

3. Tax Preference Theory

Investors prefer capital gains because:

  • Capital gains are taxed later (tax deferral benefit)
  • Often taxed at a lower rate than dividends

Firms should pay lower dividends or none at all.

Dividend changes signal information about future earnings:

  • Dividend Increase → signal of strong future prospects
  • Dividend Cut → signal of trouble

Managers are cautious about changing dividend levels.

Different investors have different preferences:

  • Retirees prefer stable, high dividends
  • Growth investors prefer reinvestment and capital gains

Firms attract investors aligned with their dividend policy.

Chapter 3: Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) & The Efficient Frontier

Developed by Harry Markowitz in 1952, Modern Portfolio Theory revolutionized investing by introducing the concept of portfolio optimization — balancing risk and return through diversification.

It mathematically proves that an investor can achieve higher returns at a given level of risk by carefully selecting a mix of uncorrelated assets.

Part 1: Assumptions of Modern Portfolio Theory

  1. Investors are rational and risk-averse
  1. Investors prefer higher returns for a given risk
  1. Investors make decisions based on expected return and standard deviation
  1. Returns are normally distributed
  1. There are no taxes or transaction costs
  1. All investors have equal access to information
  1. Investors can borrow/lend at the risk-free rate
While these assumptions are not realistic, the theory remains a powerful framework.

Part 2: Efficient Frontier

The Efficient Frontier is a curve that represents the set of optimal portfolios that offer the highest expected return for a given level of risk.

Portfolios below the frontier are suboptimal.
Portfolios above are theoretical or unattainable.

How It Works

  • Combine various risky assets
  • Calculate expected return and standard deviation for each portfolio
  • Plot all combinations
  • The top edge of this shape = Efficient Frontier

Part 1: Intuition Behind CAPM

Investors should be rewarded for two things:

  1. Time value of money → via the risk-free rate
  1. Risk taken → via market risk premium × Beta

If you take more market-related risk (higher β), you should earn more.

Chapter 7: Behavioral Finance & Psychological Biases

Traditional finance assumes that investors are rational, risk-averse, and always seek to maximize returns.

Behavioral Finance says: “That’s not how humans really behave.”

Instead, people are driven by emotion, cognitive errors, and heuristics that often lead to poor investment decisions.

Part 1: What is Behavioral Finance?

Behavioral Finance blends psychology and finance to explain how people make financial decisions that deviate from logic.

It helps us understand why investors overreact, panic, follow the crowd, or hold losing stocks too long.

Chapter 4: Portfolio Construction & Rebalancing

Portfolio construction is the process of choosing specific investments and assigning weights to create a balanced, risk-adjusted, goal-aligned portfolio.

Part 1: Portfolio Construction Process

The 5 steps of constructing a portfolio:

  1. Define objectives & constraints (from client profiling)
  1. Select asset classes (strategic allocation)
  1. Determine weights (based on return/risk expectations)
  1. Choose securities within asset classes
  1. Implement, monitor, and rebalance

Part 2: Approaches to Portfolio Construction

1. Top-Down Approach

Start with macroeconomic trends → pick asset classes → then individual securities.

Used by institutional managers, endowments, family offices.

2. Bottom-Up Approach

Start with stock-picking based on company fundamentals, regardless of sector/market.

Used by fundamental equity investors, active managers.

3. Core-Satellite Strategy

  • Core portfolio: passive/index-based (e.g., S&P 500 ETF)
  • Satellite: actively managed strategies (e.g., thematic, emerging markets)

This combines stability with alpha-seeking potential.

Part 3: Position Sizing

Position sizing answers:

How much of the portfolio should be invested in each security or asset?

Factors to consider:

  • Volatility (more volatile → smaller position)
  • Correlation with rest of portfolio
  • Client constraints (e.g., no more than 5% in one stock)
  • Risk parity or equal-risk contribution

Simple Example:

You manage PKR 100M.

  • 60% Equities (PKR 60M)
  • Within equities:
    • 30% Pakistan Stock Index ETF = PKR 18M
    • 30% US Tech ETF = PKR 18M
    • 40% Individual Global Stocks = PKR 24M

Each stock in the 40% sleeve might have max 5% of total portfolio = PKR 5M max.

Part 4: Implementation Strategies

StrategyDescription

Passive InvestingUse index funds or ETFs to match market

Part 5: Portfolio Rebalancing

Rebalancing = restoring the portfolio to its original asset allocation.

Why rebalance?

  • Markets move — allocations drift
  • Manage risk
  • Lock in gains or reinvest into undervalued assets
  • Maintain IPS compliance

Rebalancing Methods

MethodDescription
Calendar-BasedRebalance at regular intervals (e.g., quarterly)
Threshold-BasedRebalance when a position exceeds a set % (e.g., ±5%)
HybridCombination of both

Rebalancing Example

Initial target:

  • 60% Equities
  • 40% Bonds

After 6 months:

  • Equities rise → now 70%
  • Bonds fall → now 30%

Action: Sell equities, buy bonds to restore 60/40 balance.

Rebalancing Costs

  • Transaction costs (spreads, commissions)
  • Taxes (capital gains)
  • Market impact (for large portfolios)

Good asset managers balance precision with cost-efficiency.

Part 6: Common Mistakes in Portfolio Construction

  • Over-diversification (too many positions = index-like results)
  • Ignoring correlation (owning 10 banks = false diversification)
  • Lack of rebalancing
  • Performance-chasing
  • Ignoring fees and taxes
  • Misaligning with client risk profile

Chapter 5: Performance Evaluation & Risk Attribution

Measuring how well a portfolio performs is central to professional asset management. Clients need to know:

  • Did the portfolio meet its objectives?
  • Was the manager skilled or just lucky?
  • Where did returns come from — market movements or active decisions?

Part 1: Portfolio Return Calculation

1. Holding Period Return (HPR)

Where:

  • P0P_0P0​﻿ = Beginning price
  • P1P_1P1​﻿ = Ending price
  • D = Dividends/Income

2. Time-Weighted Return (TWR)

  • Eliminates the effect of external cash flows
  • Ideal for comparing managers
  • Reflects the compound rate of return on assets over time

3. Money-Weighted Return (MWR)

  • Takes cash flows (in/out) into account
  • Equivalent to IRR (Internal Rate of Return)
  • Reflects client’s experience of performance

Part 2: Benchmarking Performance

Performance means nothing without a benchmark.

What is a Benchmark?

A comparable portfolio or index that reflects:

  • The same asset classes
  • The same risk level
  • The same investment universe

Part 3: Risk-Adjusted Return Metrics

It’s not enough to just earn high returns — we must understand how much risk was taken to achieve them.

1. Sharpe Ratio

Where:

  • RpR_pRp​﻿ = Portfolio return
  • RfR_fRf​﻿ = Risk-free rate
  • σp\sigma_pσp​﻿ = Portfolio standard deviation
Measures return per unit of total risk. Higher is better.

2. Treynor Ratio

  • Uses beta (systematic risk) instead of total volatility
  • Good for diversified portfolios

3. Sortino Ratio

Like Sharpe, but only considers downside volatility, not total standard deviation.

More realistic for risk-averse investors.

4. Information Ratio

  • Measures skill in generating alpha relative to volatility of that alpha
  • Used to compare active managers
